Learning to Ship Quicker

March 2013

The redesign of this site has been a really long time coming. What I mean by that is I’ve been stalling for over a year. I’ve been learning to ship quicker however, and I’m going to share a bit about what inspired me.

For context, I’ve been building websites since I was 13. I can crank out a site like this in a few weeks for a client. Yet I found myself in this ridiculous (and counter-productive) feedback loop that oscillated between “I really need to get this done,” and “Why the hell isn’t it done yet?” I wasn’t even just working slowly, I was completely stalled. Learning about Lean UX and outcome-centric thinking is what helped me learn to ship quicker, and I want to share some of those resources.

@zachalbert yeah, the curse of designers on personal projects. I have a few of my own I still need to finish :/

— Matt Johnston (@mattsjohnston) March 13, 2013

Yeah…

I’m not sure why I’m so susceptible to procrastinating on my own projects, but it’s a nasty little mental block. Before I mention what changed for me, it’s important to note that I still really suck at it — I’m just getting better.

The first thing that changed was simply recognizing how badly I was procrastinating. Nothing like keeping tabs on the popular shots on Dribbble to make you feel like a slacker. Keep that crap to a minimum though, lest you start to equate popularity on Dribbble with success at solving real world problems for non-designer people (which is hopefully why you’re doing design).

The second — and biggest — thing that changed was getting inspired to build things by other people who have learned to ship quickly.

Learn to Focus on Outcomes

I spend my daylight hours building web apps with a rapid-fire, Lean UX approach. Lean UX preaches rapid prototyping, iteration, and customer validation. The tough part is that customer validation in this context means getting approval from the worst client ever — yourself. Part of being a good designer means staying thirsty to improve things around you, and we tend to apply this philosophy most ruthlessly to our own projects. This is actually a great thing as long as we can stay focused on outcomes instead of outputs. A plainer way to state that is to figure out what your end goal is, and drive at it uncompromisingly.

I knew that in order to implement this perspective shift, I needed to do something drastically different. Inspired by Drew Wilson and Josh Long, who wrote a book about rapid execution in 8 days (naturally), I shifted into survival mode.

Survival mode forces a rapid realignment of priorities. Everything that isn’t directly related to survival is ignored, freeing up all your resources to pursue what’s important. What’s important in this case are the outcomes you hope to achieve.

Outcomes are reasonable things, completely separate from deliverables. “I want to launch my website redesign” is an output. This type of thinking will lead to disappointment and procrastination, because it’s hard to tell what success looks like. An outcome-centric way to modify that statement might be “I want to share what I’m learning about doing UX design for startups with my design colleagues and startup founders.”

Notice there’s no implementation assumptions in that last statement. It could be a website redesign, but doesn’t necessarily have to be. Once I shifted into survival mode, asking questions in the right way helped me figure out what I should cut from the scope and what I should keep. The best way to get comfortable with this barebones approach is to make sure to log every idea and bug somewhere permanent (some people use their brains — I use Trello and Evernote).

Why You Should Care

So why is it so important to learn how to ship quickly? This is so obvious it hardly bears repeating, but here it is anyway: The web is a continuously evolving medium. There is an impermanence to it like nothing else, and the act of contributing changes it forever. Frank Chimero sums up what it means to work in the world of web & design better than anyone else:

“Culture changes what it expects from design after design changes culture.”Frank Chimero, from The Shape of Design, chapter 6.

In other words, you know that awesome thing you’ve been building for the last year? Well… it’s already 4.7 internet years out of date.

The hardest part about all this is knowing exactly what to cut, and when it’s appropriate to do so. This takes practice, but outcome-centric thinking really helps.
